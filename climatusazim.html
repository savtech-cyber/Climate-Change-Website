Climate Change: The U.S. and Zimbabwe


America

U.S. wildfires burned more than 10 million acres in 2015, the largest annual amount since 20151

In 2014, U.S. greenhouse gas emissions totaled 6,870 million metric tons, a 7% increase from 19901

Since 1901, the average surface temperature across the contiguous 48 states has risen at an average rate of 0.14°F per decade, with average temperatures rising faster more recently1

In the U.S. the North, the West, and Alaska have seen temperatures increase the most1

Precipitation in the contiguous 48 states has increased at a rate of 0.17 inches per decade1

Nevada, along with other south-western states, has seen an increase in average temperature between its long term average (1895-2015) and its average temperature between 2000 and 20151

Since 1895, the last decade in the south-west of the U.S. has seen the most persistent droughts1

Summary:

Clearly, climate change is gradually, but persistently, affecting the U.S. Whether it’s the droughts, the wildfires, or just the increased temperature, these changes will affect you and change your normal way of life.



Zimbawe:

The average annual surface temperature in Zimbabwe since 1900 has increased by 0.4 C2

The five warmest years on record have occurred since 19872

The total amount of rainfall during a rainy season has decreased about 5% since 19002

There has also been an increase in rainfall variability2

A case study in Muzarabani, Zimbabwe, describes the already noticeable effects of climate change in the area. Community members claim that the climate in the area is becoming drier, and droughts and floods are common2

By 2050 and until the end of the century there will be:

A decrease in total amount of rainfall2

Changes to the beginning and end of the seasons2

More frequent and longer mid-season dry periods2

Reduced groundwater recharge2

Erratic rainfall distribution across the country2

Temperature increase between 1C and 3C (greater than the global average)2

In Runde and Mzingwane rainfall could decrease by anywhere between 12% and 16% by 20502

Summary:

Climate change is changing the weather patterns across the globe, including in Zimbabwe. Due to the increase in rainfall variability it makes it difficult for people who depend on this rainfall, such as farmers, to plan their activities. Members of the Muzarabani community have already begun putting protective measures in place, such as farming drought-tolerant grains, flood-proof building designs, and a community grain store for in times of need.
